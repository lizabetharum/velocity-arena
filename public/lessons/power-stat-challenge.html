Section 1 · The Formula

Charge Speed = Power²

Your bot charges at full speed — but only if your Power stat is high enough. The formula tells you exactly how fast.

The Rule
Charge Speed = Power2
If Power = 4 → chargePower = 4 × 4 = 16%  |  If Power = 8 → chargePower = 8 × 8 = 64%
📖
Squared (²) means multiply a number by itself.
4² = 4 × 4 = 16.   8² = 8 × 8 = 64.   It is not the same as doubling (4 × 2 = 8). Squaring is much bigger.

How the Cutebot Pro Knows When to Charge

In a real match, your bot doesn't wait for you to press a button. It uses its ultrasonic sensor — the two eyes on the front — to measure the distance to the ball. When the ball is within 70 cm, the charge triggers automatically.

Key formula: chargePower = min(motorPower + POWER², 100). With SPEED = 0, motorPower = 0, so this reduces to just POWER². Power 4 → 16%. Power 8 → 64%. The math you did on paper matches what runs on the bot — as long as Speed stays at 0.

Troubleshooting — Bot not behaving?
Bot doesn't move at POWER = 4 (chargePower 16% stalls) Some Cutebot Pro units stall at very low motor speeds. Try POWER = 5 (chargePower = 25%) and note the adjustment. The ratio math still holds.
Sensor reads 0 or "no charge" on button A The sonar returns 0 when nothing is detected, or a large number when the ball is too far. Make sure the ball is on TAPE 2 (within 40 cm of the bot nose) before pressing A.
Ball rolls sideways instead of straight The bot's approach angle is off. Center the bot exactly on TAPE 1. Measure the straight-line distance from TAPE 2 rather than the ball's curved path.
Power 8 result is more than 4× Power 4 result Something shifted in your setup. Reset both tape marks, re-run once, and document what changed. You only get one re-run — imperfect data is still usable if you note it.

Section 4 · Reflect

Connect the Math to the Match

Your formula predicted one number. The bot produced another. Now decide what that means.

The Key Question

You measured a gap between the formula's prediction and what the bot actually did.
Is that gap a problem — or is it information?

Think again → Did you follow the setup exactly — same tape marks, same reset every run? If yes, the gap didn't come from your error. It came from something the formula doesn't know about. What might the formula be missing?
Exactly right. The formula models the motor code — it says nothing about real-world physics. Friction, acceleration time, ball weight — those aren't in the formula. The gap is evidence. Engineers use gaps to find what their models don't account for yet.
Why doesn't the physical ratio match 1:4?

Reason 1: Motor code setting ≠ physical velocity. The MakeCode speed setting (16%, 64%) is a percentage of the Cutebot Pro's maximum motor power — not a physical speed. Over a 40 cm run starting from rest, the bot is still accelerating when it hits the ball. The ratio of contact velocities between the two runs is not 1:4, because the acceleration dynamics over a short fixed distance are not proportional to the code setting.

Reason 2: Rolling friction is not linear. Ball travel distance does not scale linearly with contact velocity. A ball struck twice as hard does not roll twice as far. Rolling friction absorbs energy at a rate that is not proportional to speed.

The result: your formula models the code. The physical world involves additional physics the formula doesn't account for. Charge speed goes 1:4 in code, but ball distance only goes about 1:2 to 1:3 — and the absolute distances stay small because the bot is decelerating after contact and the ball rolls on a surface with friction. That gap is the evidence.
