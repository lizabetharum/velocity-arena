The Distance Formula
The shortest path between two points on a grid
Key Words
Coordinate
Two numbers that name a spot on a grid — written (x, y). Example: (4, 2) means 4 across, 2 up.
Distance
How far apart two points are — measured in a straight line.
Grid Unit
One square on the field. The field is 8 units wide and 4 units tall.
√ (square root)
The opposite of squaring. √16 = 4 because 4 × 4 = 16. Your calculator has a √ button.

The Formula
Distance between two points
d = √ ( (x₂ − x₁)² + (y₂ − y₁)² )
d = the distance  ·  x₁, y₁ = first point  ·  x₂, y₂ = second point
(x₂ − x₁)²
Subtract the x-values. Then square it (multiply by itself).
(y₂ − y₁)²
Subtract the y-values. Then square it.
+ (add)
Add the two squared numbers together.
√ (root)
Take the square root of the total. That's your distance.

Common Mistake — Two Different Paths
Using x = 3 and y = 4 — same numbers, different order — different answers:
✗ Wrong Order
x = 3   y = 4
↓ add first ← ERROR
3 + 4 = 7
↓ then square
7² = 49
↓ root
√49 = 7 ✗
✓ Right Order
↓ square each first
x=3
3²=9
y=4
4²=16
↓ then add
9 + 16 = 25
↓ root
√25 = 5 ✓

Reflect — Answer as a Team
Question 1
How much of the full A→G distance did your charge close? Write it as a fraction and a percent.
A→G = ? units  ·  A→B = ? units
Fraction: A→B ÷ A→G = _____  ·  Percent: _____ %
💡 How do I write it as a fraction?
Divide: A→B ÷ A→G. Example: if A→B = 2 and A→G = 4, the fraction is 2/4 = 1/2 = 50%. Your charge closed half the distance.
Question 2
How many straight-line charges like this would theoretically be needed to score from tip-off?
Divide A→G by A→B. Round up to the nearest whole number.
Example: If A→G = 4 and A→B = 2, you need 4 ÷ 2 = 2 charges (minimum).
💡 What if I get 1?
Getting 1 means your charge is long enough to score in one shot — theoretically. Did that actually happen in your match? If not, think about why: the path may not be perfectly straight, or contact may have been off-center.
Question 3
The formula gives a straight-line distance. Why might your calculated A→B be different from where the ball actually ended up?
💡 Think about what happened during the charge
Your B is an estimate from memory — not an exact measurement. Also think about: did the scoop hit the ball in the center? Did the ball roll straight, or curve? A straight-line formula can't account for roll or off-center contact.
