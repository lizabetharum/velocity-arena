Final Proposal Verification — Facilitator Reference

VELOCITY ARENA · DAY 12 · CHALLENGE BLOCK · 9:25–10:00

35 min Day 12
Launch — 30 Seconds (9:25)
"Verification worksheet. 35 minutes. Four sections: check your two numbers against the dashboard, calculate how your rule helps the last-place team, confirm there's no conflict of interest, name the math concept type. Before we go to lunch, I'll ask one person per team to explain one calculation from memory — and name the concept. Go."

Three Pass Priorities
1
Min 1–13 · Every table
Find the number that changed during the Lobby. Verify it against the dashboard. Do not verify it for them — point and step back.
"Where in the dashboard did this number come from?" If they can't point to a row: "That number comes out. Replace it with one you can find. You have time."
2
Min 13–23 · Conflict-of-interest check
Section 3 of worksheet. Compare % change for last-place vs. their own team. If their team benefits more, redirect — don't fix it for them.
"If this rule passes, who benefits more — last-place or your team? Show me the math." If their team benefits more: "Better to catch it now than have the vote catch it."
3
Min 23–28 · Concept label
Section 4. Every proposal must name at least one concept: proportional scaling, unit rate, ratio, slope as rate of change, or linear decay. A number without a concept label fails the rubric.
"What kind of math is the calculation in your proposal?" Point to the line on the worksheet and wait.
Accountability Check (9:53) — One Student Per Team
Question 1
"How did you calculate this number?"
Pass: they name the dashboard row, describe the operation, state the result. Fail: they read from the worksheet instead of explaining from memory.
Question 2
"What kind of math is that — proportional scaling, unit rate, ratio, slope as rate of change, or linear decay?"
Pass: they name one. Fail: they can't identify it. Give them 5 min to add the concept label before the meeting.
Who to ask: Not the most vocal student. Ask someone who hasn't been driving the proposal. The goal is to confirm the team — not just one person — understands the math.
Watch For
Number isn't on the dashboard "Where in the dashboard did this come from?" If they can't point to a row, it comes out. A verified smaller number beats an unverified bigger one.
Significant error found with 10 minutes left "What's the real number? Does it still support your argument?" Stay calm. If yes: update and move. If no: "What's the strongest true number you have? Use that."
Rule primarily benefits the proposal team "Look at Section 3. Run the comparison. If your team gains more than last-place, the vote will catch it. Better to revise now." Don't tell them what to change — show them the question.
Team finishes early "Say in one sentence why the last-place team is better off under your rule than they are now — out loud." Clear sentence = ready. Vague sentence = keep working.
Wrap (9:58)
"Proposals are final. Lunch now. Commissioner's Meeting starts right after. Each team presents for exactly 3 minutes. You cite your two numbers. You explain your rule. You say how it helps the last-place team. League votes. If your proposal passes, it becomes a rule. Written on the wall. For real."
Highest Risk · Commissioner's Meeting This Afternoon
One confident team presents first, others disengage from checking math in their own arguments. Mitigate: sequence a mid-confidence team first — sets the verification norm before the dominant team presents. After every presentation, before the vote, ask the room: "Does this proposal use the data correctly? Can anyone verify the math?" Do this after every team, including the strongest one.
