The Stat System
20 points. Four stats. Every allocation is a math problem.

Each team distributes exactly 20 points across four attributes. Every point has a mathematical consequence. The stat card is binding for the duration of each season.

S
Speed
Motor Power = (S × 100) / 20
Proportional reasoning, unit rates. Double the stat, double the output. The most intuitive — and the trap that sets up the budget constraint.
E
Endurance
P(t) = 100 − (20−E) × 1.5 × t
Rate of change, linear decay. Power drops over time. students feel the decay physically before they formalize it on Day 2.
T
Turning
Arc Segments = T
Turn Ratio = 0.2 + (T × 0.02)
Coordinate geometry, distance formula. Wrong allocation means the bot misses its target coordinate — visible on the field.
P
Power
Charge Speed = P² / 2
Follow Through = P × 200 ms
Quadratic scaling, ratio. students expect linear behavior and discover non-linearity. The designed shock moment of Week 1.
S + E + T + P = 20 The "=" sign means balance — not "the answer is." This is the most common misconception to address in Week 1.
The Four-Week Arc
Discovery → Analysis → Agency → Mastery.

Each week escalates in mathematical complexity, competitive stakes, and student agency. Weeks build on each other — Week 2 is only possible because of Week 1.

Week 1 · Days 1–5
Play Lab + Boot Camp
Curious, experimental, low-stakes

One stat per day. Students get each formula into their hands before combining them. By Friday, they name their bot, design three possible loadouts, and lock in the first one.

Stat card complete · Bot named · Season 1 allocation locked
Day 1 → Day 2 → Day 3 → Day 4 → Day 5 →
Week 2 · Days 6–10
Season 1
Competitive, analytical, invested

Real match play. Before every match, write a prediction. After every match, compare it to what happened. The gap is what you work to close. The dashboard becomes central.

Dashboard full · Linear equation written · Season 2 hypothesis with one cited number
Day 6 → Day 7 → Day 8 → Day 9 → Day 10 →
Week 3 · Days 11–15
Season 2 + Commissioner
Empowered, analytical, creative

Students run the rules. Proposals backed by real dashboard data, voted by the league. Season 2 tests whether the revised model improved. Protect student agency — over-scaffolding is the main pitfall.

Commissioner rule voted · Season 2 complete · Scouting report started
Day 11 → Day 12 → Day 13 → Day 14 → Day 15 →
Week 4 · Days 16–20
Championship + Exhibition
High-stakes, reflective, celebratory

Scouting reports, pre-match pitches, double-elimination Championship. Day 19: students teach visitors. Day 20: post-diagnostic closes the loop against Day 1.

Daily Structure
Same rhythm every day. Different math.

Every day follows this six-block structure. The structure is non-negotiable. Skipping blocks — especially ENDS — breaks the arc of the week.

Time Block Duration What Happens
10:00–10:05 Arrival + Check-In 5 min Materials out, bots on table, team huddle. No dead time.
10:05–10:30 Hook 25 min Activity that frames the day's math — improv, movement, or competition. No formula yet. Builds cognitive readiness and trust.
10:30–11:35 Challenge Block 65 min Core math instruction. Formula derivation, error analysis, problem sets, Commissioner rubric. This is where the day's concept is named, worked through, and owned.
11:35–12:35 Lunch 60 min Off-schedule. Eat, rest, talk about anything except math.
12:35–2:05 Match-Build-Make 90 min Match days: pre-match prediction → 3 matches → Loss Recovery. Build days: fabrication and code verification.
2:05–2:40 Open Lab 35 min Track A (Data Dive) or Track B (Bot Lab). Students self-select. Both cover the same math — one through numbers, one through the bot. Never assign a track.
2:40–2:55 ENDS Closing Ritual 15 min One prompt per student. Cold Recall, journal, exit ticket, or skill bridge. Runs every day without exception. Research shows this is the highest-yield daily routine for retention.
2:55–3:00 Pack-Up + Departure 5 min Materials returned, sign out.
Core Protocols
Memorize these before Day 1.

These protocols are the curriculum's connective tissue. Used consistently, they convert every match loss, emotional spike, and data gap into a math learning event.

Loss Recovery
After every match loss — within 30 seconds.
  1. 1Redirect to the dashboard immediately. "Open your data. Let's look at the numbers."
  2. 2Ask the first diagnostic question. "What stat had the biggest impact? Answer in numbers."
  3. 3Find one change supported by data. "Where would you move 2 points, and why?" Write it down.
  4. 4Test the hypothesis in Open Lab. Reprogram + test. Result goes in the dashboard.
  5. 5Name what improved, specifically, at ENDS. "Your motor power went from 20% to 30% — that's data-driven revision."
Data Dashboard
The primary artifact. Non-negotiable from Day 3.
  1. 1Record every match result immediately. No blank rows. No "we'll fill it in later."
  2. 2Formulas live in the dashboard, not on loose worksheets. Residuals, distance formula, percent change — all alongside the data they came from.
  3. 3The dashboard is public starting Day 15 — scouting is by design. Accurate entry matters.
  4. 4Spot-check during Open Lab. "What's the data for Match 2?" Don't fill it in for them.
Match Day
Pre, during, and post — in that order.
  1. PREAll bots programmed before Match-Build-Make starts. Pre-match predictions written. Yesterday's dashboard rows complete.
  2. DURINGNo coaching during matches — observe only. Intervene for safety only. Loss Recovery activates immediately for emotional distress.
  3. POSTTransfer match logs to dashboards before Open Lab. Update public leaderboard. Write Rivalry Card observation.
Open Lab Two-Track
Announce both. Never assign. Let students self-select.
  1. ATrack A — Data Dive. Analysis through numbers: graphing, equations, scatter plots, residual extension.
  2. BTrack B — Bot Lab. Analysis through the bot: reprogramming, testing, iteration. Particularly valuable for ELL students.
  3. →Teams can split between tracks. Bot Lab results must go back into the dashboard — physical results are data points too.
Facilitator Question Bank
The questions that do the work.

Memorize the first three. They cover 80% of situations.

When a student asks "is this right?"
"What does your data say?" / "Can your team verify that?"
When a team loses a match
"Open your dashboard. What did you predict? What actually happened? Name the gap."
When a team is stuck on a formula
"What formula applies here — and why?" / "Check the reference card. Try it. Then come back to me."
When progress stalls during Open Lab
"What's one thing you'd change about your loadout, and what data supports it?"
When a team finishes early
"If you could change one rule, what data from your dashboard would support that proposal?"
When a Commissioner proposal is vague
"You need two numbers from your dashboard. What are they, and what do they show?"
When a student says "we lost because our bot is bad"
"Your Speed formula gives what percentage of motor power? Is that enough to win? What does the math say?"
When a Championship pitch claim is vague
"Every claim needs a number. 'Our bot is fast' scores zero. 'Our Speed = 7 gives 35% motor power' scores full points."
Supporting Diverse Learners
Plan for these before Day 1 — they are not edge cases.

Every room of 18 students will include multiple learner profiles. The strategies below are built into the curriculum. Know them before you walk in.

Frequency: 2–4 per room
Basic Operations Gaps
Common in target population
Pair with a mid-level student, not the strongest. Allow calculator access. Focus on formula structure, not arithmetic speed. The formula work matters more than the arithmetic.
Frequency: 1–3 per camp
English Language Learner (ELL)
Math ability is independent of English proficiency
An ELL may be your strongest mathematician. Use sentence frames: "My equation predicts ___ because ___." Allow diagram + calculation in place of written explanation. Track B (Bot Lab) is especially valuable — math is visible in physical behavior.
Frequency: ≥1 per room
IEP / Learning Difference
Processing speed + attention most common
Provide a checklist version of multi-step tasks. Allow extended time during ENDS. Quiet corner for processing if the room gets loud during matches.
Frequency: Variable
Math Anxiety
Watch for Day 1 refusals
Name the specific correct thing they did. Never call on publicly unless they volunteer. The Day 1 improv activities (Sound and Movement, Mirror) have no wrong answers — use them intentionally to establish safety before any formula work.
