Facilitation Cheatsheets

Print this packet and keep it on a clipboard. The front side covers the standard Challenge Block flow; the back side covers in-the-moment differentiation moves you can use while circulating.

Standard Challenge Block Flow

Use this flow for most math-heavy sessions: formulas, worksheets, and dashboard work. Adjust minutes to match the daily schedule.

  1. 0–5 min
    Launch & connect
    Name the day’s question in bot terms (for example, “How much does Speed really change goals?”) and connect to yesterday’s match or worksheet. One sentence, then into the task.
  2. 5–10 min
    Introduce the formula or tool
    Write the formula on the board (percent change, distance, y = mx + b, or a constraint) and work one full example with students, labeling each step in words. Take one round of clarifying questions, then move on.
  3. 10–45 min
    Team work: Challenge Block
    Teams work through three loadouts, three coordinate pairs, or the full worksheet using the formula. Write visible checkpoints on the board (for example, “Loadout 1 by 15, Loadout 2 by 30, Loadout 3 + trade-off by 40”) and circulate with targeted prompts.
  4. During work
    In-the-moment moves
    Use the differentiation tree on the back: Operations Quick Reference instead of reteaching, sentence frames for writing prompts, Checker/Verifier roles for students who are behind or finish early, and extra processing time where needed.
  5. 45–55 min
    Compare & interpret
    Do a whole-room share-out using a comparison surface (class table, scatter plot, or Team Comparison Table). Ask, “Which team changed the most? In which stat? In which direction? What does that mean in bot behavior?”
  6. Last 5 min
    ENDS prompt & closing
    Have campers write or speak a short ENDS response (evidence, nuance, decision, strategy) and preview how today’s numbers feed into tomorrow’s matches or rule decisions.
Protect if short on time: One worked example, 20+ minutes of team calculation work, and a brief compare / interpret step. Trim warm-up and number of share-out voices first.

In-the-Moment Differentiation Tree

Start with what you see at a table, then follow the move. Each move keeps the math at full rigor while removing barriers for that camper.

Stuck on arithmetic or operations

Pauses at 6 ÷ 20, adds instead of subtracting in parentheses, or avoids writing steps and only writes final answers.

  1. First move: Quietly point to the Operations Quick Reference at their station. Name the operation in one sentence (for example, “Six divided by twenty. Put 6 on top, 20 on the bottom.”) and move on.
  2. If still stuck: Have a teammate explain one calculation aloud while the camper writes every step. Keep them doing all three loadouts; do not reduce quantity or give a calculator.
  3. Avoid: Re-teaching all of division on the spot or letting a strong teammate do every calculation for them.

Math is fine, writing or vocabulary is blocked

Correct calculations but blank “write one sentence” prompts, copies a teammate’s sentence, or points at the prompt asking, “What does this mean?”

  1. First move: Offer a sentence frame that matches the prompt structure (for example, “When I doubled Speed from __ to __, the distance went from __ to __. That is / is not double because __.”).
  2. For vocabulary: Point to the term on the Math Vocabulary Wall (proportional, slope, budget) and, if needed, give one everyday synonym like “steepness,” then step back.
  3. For journals / exhibition: Allow responses in a first language with a one-sentence English summary; pair explanations with visuals such as diagrams or cards.

Finishes well before the team

Completes calculations 10–15 minutes early, begins directing others, or shifts only to bot building or social roles.

  1. First move: Hand an extension prompt card tied to the same content (for example, “Find the Speed / Power combination that maximizes output subject to S + P = 14.”). Present it as the next question, not extra credit.
  2. On match days: Assign Model Tracker or Math Verifier roles: they write the full prediction chain and check other teams’ proposal math in real time.
  3. Avoid: Using them as a permanent tutor who does math for others; the goal is deeper math for them, not faster completion.

Understands ideas but cannot keep pace

Consistently last to finish even when correct, shuts down during timed checks, or drifts off mid-Challenge Block.

  1. First move: Make checkpoints visible on the board (for example, “Loadout 1 by minute 15, Loadout 2 by 30, Loadout 3 by 40”), and assign a Checker / Verifier role during team work.
  2. Timed events: During Cold Recall Checks, quietly allow an extra three minutes and collect their paper last. During Formula Relay, have them verify one card per round instead of racing to solve every card.
  3. Digit reversal / transcription: Ask them to say the number aloud before writing. If they say “thirty-five” and write 53, point at the mismatch and have them correct it.
Reminder: Profiles can overlap. A camper may need both a sentence frame and an extension prompt in the same session.
