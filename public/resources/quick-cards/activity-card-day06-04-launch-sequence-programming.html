Launch Sequence Programming + Testing
4 of 6
Day 6 · Match-Build-Make · 60 min
Follows Field Geometry
MATH: Hit rate = hits ÷ 3 trials per strategy · Compare Strategy A vs. B on consistency, accuracy, and post-kick position · One-variable revision in Step 8
Before You Start
  • Write on board: Ball at (4, 2). Launch = your best chance to score.
  • Distribute one worksheet per team
  • Robots charged — 6+ live trials this block
  • Phase 1 is the only controlled moment. Post-kick position shapes Phase 2 coverage.
Students Produce
  • Two coded strategies: A (Direct Hit) and B (Buffered Approach)
  • 6 trial rows — Hit Ball + Good Kick recorded (3 per strategy)
  • Consistency scores: A hits/3 and B hits/3
  • Post-kick position for each strategy (Step 5)
  • One strategy chosen with 3-part justification + Step 8 revision recorded
Justification Standard (Steps 6–7)
Which strategy + direct or buffered
Hit rate number (out of 3 trials)
Post-kick position + why it helps Phase 2
✗ "It hits better." / "It's faster."
✗ Any reason without a hit-rate number
Step 8 — one variable only
Turn angle · Drive time · Drive distance
One run, record result. Two variables = can't isolate cause.
Timing
0:00 Frame Phase 1 vs. 2 Launch = only moment of precise control. Phase 2 is roaming — they don't control it. Pause before moving on.
3:00 Step 1: Design on paper Both strategies on paper before opening MakeCode. If A and B look identical: "One is not a buffered approach."
10:00 Code A → test A · 3 trials Code A only. Code freeze at 13:00. Demo "good kick." Same start position every run. Failed trial = ☐ no — record it.
21:00 Code B → test B · 3 trials Code B, same position as A. If B goes 0/3: record it — that's the data. No rerunning to fix a result.
33:00 Steps 3–5: Scores + post-kick Step 5 is most skipped: "Where does bot end up — left, right, center? That's where Phase 2 starts." Different angles = different post-kick positions.
38:00 Steps 6–7: Choose + justify 3-part justification (Col 1). If stuck: "What's your hit rate? Where did the bot land?" Those are the two answers.
43:00 Step 8: One variable One change only — turn angle, drive time, or drive distance. One run. Negative result is valid. Hold the rule.
54:00 Step 9 + share out "A good launch is not just about hitting once. It is about ___." 2–3 teams. Listen for consistency + post-kick.
58:00 Log check · Hard stop 6 trial rows · scores · justification · Step 8 recorded. Blank justification: say it aloud, write one word per part.
Say This
Open
"Phase 1 is the launch — you control it. Phase 2 is roaming — you don't. Today you find out which launch actually works."
Step 5
"After your robot kicks, it hits a wall and starts roaming. Left, right, or center? That's where Phase 2 begins. Check the box for each strategy."
Step 9
"A good launch is not just about hitting the ball once. It is about ___."
Not This
No tinkering between Steps 2 and 8. No justification without a hit-rate number. No two-variable changes in Step 8. Failed trial = record it, don't re-run.
Watch For → Redirect
A and B look identical "One is not a buffered approach — paths need to be visibly different." Catch before they open MakeCode.
Team opens stat loadout block "That block is closed. Turns and drives only." Redirect every time, no exceptions.
Two variables in Step 8 "Pick one — which variable is causing the miss?" Hold the rule. Two changes = no usable answer.
0/3 on both strategies Go straight to Step 8: "One variable — what's causing the miss?" A 0/3 with a hypothesis beats a 2/3 they can't explain.
Hard Stop 6 trial rows filled · consistency scores circled · justification written · Step 8 recorded. Step 8 is the minimum — do not skip it for time. Step 9 can carry as verbal exit ticket.
