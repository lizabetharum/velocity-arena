Before Block Starts
  • Write on board and leave up all block:
    P(t) = 100 − r·t    r = (20 − E) × 1.5
  • Endurance Decay Worksheets at every seat (Parts 1–3)
  • Stat cards on tables — students need their personal E value
  • Worked example: E=6 → r=21 → P(t)=100−21t → x-intercept ≈ 4.76 min
Math Context (LO 2.2)

Students have calculated P(t) values (Day 2) but never written the full slope-intercept equation. Today: name the form, calculate personal r, find x-intercept.

The x-intercept is match-relevant: a team whose bot hits zero at minute 2.1 feels that in their first match. Recording it before the match creates an evidence chain for Season 1.

Students Produce
  • Personal decay equation: P(t) = 100 − r·t (with their E value)
  • X-intercept = 100 ÷ r (minute bot hits zero)
  • Team comparison: whose bot runs longest in a 3-min match?
  • Data log entry: equation + x-intercept before first match

Watch For — Common Errors
Skipping the subtraction: r = E × 1.5 "If Endurance = 15, what does your bot do? Does that r make sense?" Make them trace it.
Confusing r with E: P(t) = 100 − E·t "What did you use for r? Where did that come from?" Trace back to board formula.
Stuck on x-intercept algebra "What value of t makes 100 − rt = 0? What do you need to do to isolate t?" Walk away — do not solve it.
X-intercept larger than 3 min (not an error) "Your bot doesn't hit zero in a 3-min match. What's your power at exactly t = 3?" Send to Reflect Q2.
Share-Out Script
Say "Two teams — one high-E, one low-E. Read your complete equation and your x-intercept. Nothing else."
After "Different allocations, different slopes, different x-intercepts. Same match clock. That gap is the whole point of the stat."
Data Log "Write your equation and your x-intercept in the gray box. That data goes in your match log before your first match."
