Finger Speed-Sums
1 of 4
20 Min · Hook Block · Day 4 · AM
Opening Activity
Before You Start
  • Desks or chairs cleared — students standing with space to move between partners
  • Ready to handle odd numbers: trio = 2 play, 1 keeps score, rotate
  • Know what "cognitive load" means before you walk in — you'll use the term after the debrief, not before
Students Produce
  • Spoken answer to one debrief question
  • Named awareness of their speed-vs-accuracy trade-off
  • Heard the bridge: "faster ≠ proportionally more output"
3 Debrief Questions
Q
Ask
1
Who changed strategy between rounds? What changed?
2
Did going faster help you score more? When did speed cost you?
3
The math is the simplest you know. Why did speed make it harder?
Don't define "cognitive load" first. Let students name the idea, then give it the term.
Timing
0–1Frame It "Start with a game. Six minutes to play. Spend those six minutes paying attention to what happens inside your head — not just whether you win."
1–3Demo Pick one student. Two rounds: one clean win, one hesitation. "What happened on that second one?" Take one answer. Don't over-explain. Move on.
3–6Round 1 Partners. 90-sec timer. Circulate: watch for blurting wrong answers, hesitation patterns. "Stop. Count your points. New partner."
6–9Round 2 New partner. 90 seconds. Watch whether anyone changed strategy after Round 1 — slowing down, being more deliberate.
9–12Round 3 Last round. 90 seconds. Watch for rhythm vs. still-blurting. "Stop. Sit down."
12–18Debrief Three questions. Pause after each. 2–3 responses each. Let students respond to each other before you move on. Don't rush.
18–20Bridge "More points in a stat does not always mean proportionally more output. We're about to find out how far off that intuition is." Move directly to Power Allocation.
Say This
Frame
"Pay attention to what happens inside your head — not just whether you win."
After demo
"What just happened on that second one? We both second-guessed ourselves."
Debrief Q3
"The math is the simplest you know. Two numbers, zero to ten. Why did speed make it feel harder?"
Bridge
"More points in a stat does not always mean proportionally more output."
Not this
Don't explain "cognitive load" before the game. The term lands harder after they've described it themselves.
Watch For → Redirect
Blurting wrong answers Don't flag during play. Let the debrief surface the wrong-answer penalty.
Hesitation in demo More useful than the clean win — signals this isn't pure reflex.
Strategy changes Slowing down, letting partner go first. Acknowledge without evaluating.
Student looks frustrated That's productive. Let it carry into the debrief — don't diffuse it early.
