Failure Bow
1 of 8
Day 2 · Hook Block · 15 min
Follows Slow Down Slow Down (Day 1 close)
MATH: No formula today — this is the mindset block. Productive failure is the prerequisite for every gap sentence this season.
Before You Start
  • Clear floor space — enough room for everyone to stand in a circle
  • Visible timer ready (phone or clock on board)
  • No materials needed
  • You go first, every time. A facilitator who models before asking earns the room. A head-nod doesn't work — make it theatrical.
Students Produce
  • No written artifact
  • Theatrical bow + verbal failure statement — one per student, two rounds
Why This Works

Some students have spent years in classrooms where a wrong answer means failure. This block retrains that reflex before the math starts.

A student who bows for a mistake on Day 2 will write a gap sentence on Day 4 without defending it. That's the transfer you're building.

Round 2 is faster and sillier by design. The contrast — Round 1 hesitation vs. Round 2 speed — is the debrief.

Timing
0:00You modelStand. Say: "I failed at ___." Bow deeply — hold 3 seconds — rise. One sentence, one bow. Make it count.
0:30Round 1"Your turn. Go around the circle. One failure, one bow. 30 seconds max per person." Start the visible timer.
8:00Round 2Same circle, faster pace. Bows get sillier. You set the tone — exaggerate yours. Don't narrate; just go.
13:00Debrief"What changed between Round 1 and Round 2?" Let them answer. Then: "What does that have to do with math class?"
14:30Bridge"Today your formulas might predict something wrong. Your bot might surprise you. That's not failure — that's data."
0:30Timer rule30 seconds max per person. Visible timer. Call next person at 30s even if they're mid-sentence.
Say This
Model
"I failed at remembering a student's name last week. [bow] That's it. Your turn."
Debrief
"Round 1 was slow. Round 2 was fast. Why? Because the bow made failure a performance — a choice. You decided to do it."
Bridge
"We're going to need that today. Your formula might be off. Your bot might do something unexpected. That's data, not a problem."
Not this
Don't ask "Does anyone want to go first?" — you go first every time. Don't accept a head-nod as a bow. Don't explain the activity before modeling it.
Watch For → Redirect
Student freezes, won't participate"Failure can be tiny. 'I was late this morning' works." Say it once, then move to the next person and come back.
Student turns it into comedy — disrupts the roomLet one joke land. On the second: "Good. Next person — something real." Keep moving.
Student takes more than 30 secondsCall time out loud. "Next person." No apology, no explanation. The timer is the rule.
Student asks "What does this have to do with robots?""Good question. Hold it. You'll have an answer by the end of today." Then move on.
Hard Stop 30 seconds per person, enforced with visible timer. Call next even mid-sentence.
