Driving Question Reveal
3 of 9
Day 1 · Hook Block · 10 min
Follows Mirror Pairs
PURPOSE: Anchor the full program to a single driving question before students touch any math or materials. The question is the hook — over-explaining kills it.
Before You Start
  • Large poster — covered or face-down — where the entire room can read it once revealed
  • Teams pre-assigned and recorded on a card or board students cannot see yet
  • Seats arranged in groups of 3, tagged by team number
  • Do all of this before students arrive. Do not assign teams on the fly during the reveal.
  • Do not preview the program or over-explain the question. The question is the hook.
Students Produce
  • Driving question written on the board — class product, stays up for the session
  • Optional brief written response if facilitator prompts individual reflection
Timing
0:00Reveal"Before you sit with your team, I want to show you what you're working toward." Uncover the poster. Read aloud, slowly. Then hold silence for 10 seconds — do not fill it.
0:02Team formationCall names at a steady pace. No editorializing. Just names and team numbers. Once seated: "Look at the two people next to you. You don't need to know each other yet."
0:07Introduction taskGive 3 minutes exactly. Walk the room — don't stand at the front. At 2 minutes, give a quiet signal. At 3 minutes, call it with the close line.
0:10Move onDon't linger. "We'll do the math tomorrow. Today was about showing up." Move directly to Stat Card Distribution.
Say This
Reveal
"How can you use data, equations, and code to build a robot athlete that wins on math, not luck?"
After silence
"You don't need to answer that right now. Every dataset you build, every equation you write, every line of code — it connects back to that question."
Intro task
"Three minutes. Tell each other one thing you already know how to do — code, math, build, anything — and one thing you've never done before."
Close
"We'll do the math tomorrow. Today was about showing up. You did that."
Not this
Don't explain the question further after the reveal. Don't preview the program. Don't negotiate team assignments — "Teams are set. You'll understand why later."
Watch For → Redirect
Student lobbies to change teams"Teams are set. You'll understand why later." Don't negotiate. Don't explain.
Student looks isolated during introductionsWalk over. Ask directly: "What's one thing you know how to do?" Give a clear entry point before moving on.
Energy drops after the revealExpected. Don't try to pump it back up. The weight of a real question is different from the weight of confusion. Let the quiet be productive.
Students ask about robots immediately"Good questions. Hold them. We start tomorrow." Move on.
