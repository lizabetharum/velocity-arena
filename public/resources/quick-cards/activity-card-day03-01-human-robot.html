Human Robot Programming Challenge
1 of 3
Day 3 · Hook Block · 25 min
Follows Day 2 Close
MATH: No formula — precision and units. The ambiguous instruction is the setup for every MakeCode block that follows.
Before You Start
  • Two floor spots taped per team: start and destination, 4–5 moves apart
  • Teams standing near their spots before the block opens
  • No materials needed
  • You model ambiguity first — "Forward 2" — and let the robot go the wrong way before explaining why. Don't correct the robot. Let the mistake land.
Students Produce
  • No written artifact
  • Verbal answers to 3 debrief questions — especially Q3 (what MakeCode does with ambiguous code) left open
Round Key
Round 1 — 4 min. First robot. Programmers give one instruction at a time. Robot executes exactly as given. Ambiguity shows up immediately.
Round 2 — 5 min. Rotate. New robot. Watch whether teams overcorrect (over-specify) or get precision right.
Round 3 — 3 min. Optional blindfold. Final rotation.
The key transfer: MakeCode is the same kind of robot. It executes exactly what the code says. Leave that question open — the afternoon block answers it.
Timing
0:00Frame it"The robot follows every instruction exactly as given. Not what you meant. Exactly what you said. No touching. No pointing. Words only."
1:00I DoPick one student as your robot. Say "Forward 2." Let ambiguity play out — don't correct. "Did the robot do what I intended? The robot isn't wrong. The instruction is. '2 what?'"
3:00Assign roles"Programmers: one instruction at a time. Robot: follow exactly. If unclear, pick an interpretation and go. Don't ask. Just execute."
8:00Round 1 — 4 minCirculate without intervening. Let robots go the wrong way. Note which instructions cause problems. Call time at 12:00.
12:00Round 2 — 5 min"Rotate. New robot. Same rules." Watch for over-specification. Note what you see. Call time at 17:00.
17:00Round 3 — 3 min"Last rotation." Optional blindfold. Call time at 22:00.
22:00Debrief — 3 min(1) What happened when instructions were ambiguous? (2) What did you have to add to make one work? (3) If MakeCode says "turn left" with no degree value, what does the bot do? Leave Q3 open.
Say This
Frame
"The robot follows every instruction exactly as given. Not what you meant. Not what you were trying to say. Exactly what you said."
After demo
"The robot did exactly what I said. I said 'Forward 2.' Two what? The robot isn't wrong. The instruction is."
Debrief Q2
"What did you have to add? What made the difference between a bad instruction and a good one?" Listen for: units, direction, reference point, degrees. Don't supply the words.
Not this
Don't correct the robot during the activity. Don't resolve Debrief Q3 — the afternoon answers it. Don't let teams agree on a unit system in advance without acknowledging what would have happened if they hadn't.
Watch For → Redirect
Teams agree on a unit before Round 1"What if you hadn't agreed first?" The agreement is a lesson about shared conventions.
Robot asks for clarification mid-instruction"Don't ask. Pick an interpretation and go." MakeCode can't ask either.
Round 2 teams over-specify every stepLet it play out — verbose, fragile programs are the other extreme. Note it for debrief.
Debrief Q3 gets answered quickly"Hold that thought. You'll see what actually happens this afternoon."
