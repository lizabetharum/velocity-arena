Follows Interleaved Problem Set
MATH: Distance formula — d = √( (x₂ − x₁)² + (y₂ − y₁)² ) · Applied twice per team: P₁ and P₂ vs. ball zone at (4, 2)
Before You Start
  • Write on board: Ball zone A = (4, 2)  ·  Goal = (8, 2)
  • Post or project the coordinate grid from the worksheet
  • Confirm every team has Speed (S) and Turning (T) from their stat loadout
  • Distribute one worksheet per team
  • Robots charged and ready — Step 9 requires a live test run
  • Key framing: stat loadout sets bot performance. Starting position is match strategy — a separate decision. Hold this before students arrive.
Students Produce
  • Two distance calculations (P₁ and P₂ vs. ball zone at (4, 2))
  • Wall risk + arc coverage judgment for each position (Steps 4–6)
  • Final starting position with written justification: (x, y) + a number + a strategic reason
  • Observed test result vs. Step 2 prediction (Step 10)
Justification Standard
d = √( (x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)² )
Ball zone always (x₂, y₂) = (4, 2). Worked example on worksheet uses Start = (5, 6). Students walk through it before their own calculation.
✓ (x, y) + calculated distance + strategic reason
✓ "We chose (1, 2): 3 units from center vs. 4.1 for P₂. Far from wall — robot reaches ball zone without hitting a wall on the first arc."
✗ "It feels right." / "It's closer."
✗ Any reason with no calculated number
Timing
Phase 1 — Frame + Choose Starting Positions (5 min)
0:00 Frame the problem "Your robot moves in curved arcs, bounces off walls, changes direction. Where you start changes the whole match — not just the first moment."
1:30 Steps 1–2: Choose + predict Pick two candidate positions and predict which performs better before calculating. If a team tries (4,2) or (8,2): "That's the ball zone or goal — pick open field."
Phase 2 — Calculate Distance to Center (10 min)
5:00 Step 3: Distance formula Write formula on board. Both P₁ and P₂ required before moving to Step 4. Common errors: squaring before subtracting; calculating only one position; flipped coordinates.
Phase 3 — Evaluate + Decide (8 min)
15:00 Steps 4–6: Wall risk, coverage, early behavior Judgment steps — no single right answer. Hold for reasoning. High Turning = tight arcs, favor edge starts. Low Turning = wide sweeps, favor center. Step 6: push teams to choose one checkbox, not circle all three.
19:00 Steps 7–8: Decision + justification Commit to one position. Justification = (x, y) + a number + a strategic reason. If stuck: "What did your distance calculation show? Why does that number matter for the match?"
Phase 4 — Test + Reflect (5 min)
23:00 Steps 9–10: Run bot, observe, adjust "Watch what the robot actually does — not just the ball. Where does it go in the first 10 seconds?" Prediction mismatch is the point. Accept any adjustment connected to observation.
Phase 5 — Turning Analysis + Synthesis (5 min)
28:00 Steps 11–13: Arc width + coverage shape "Your Turning value is set. Wide or tight arcs? Does your starting position match what Turning predicts?" Don't correct mismatches — let teams find them.
31:00 Steps 14–16: Synthesis Connect position + Turning + Speed in one statement. Name a failure case (e.g. high Turning + edge start). Match Impact checkbox. Move fast — these can carry into Open Lab.
33:00 Share out "Closest start to ball zone? Lowest wall risk? Did your test match your prediction?" Listen for position + Turning + arc width connected as one system.
35:00 Hard stop Steps 1–10 must be complete for all teams. Steps 11–13 for most. Steps 14–16 can carry into next Open Lab.
Say This
Open
"Your robot does not drive straight to the goal. It moves in curved arcs, bounces off walls. Where you start changes what happens over the whole match — not just the first moment. Pick a starting position that keeps your robot in open space and passes through the center where the ball is likely to be."
Formula
"Your starting point is (x₁, y₁). The ball zone is always (x₂, y₂) = (4, 2). Walk through the worked example on the worksheet before you calculate your own."
Test Run
"Run it from your starting position. Watch what the robot actually does — don't just watch the ball. Where does it go in the first 10 seconds?"
Not This
Don't suggest which position is best. Don't accept justifications without a calculated number. Don't correct a mismatch between their chosen position and their arc prediction — let them find it.
Watch For → Redirect
Team reverts to straight-line / approach-angle thinking "The robot moves in curved paths. Your Turning value controls the curve. Start from that." Don't re-explain the earlier activity — that framing no longer applies.
Team calculates distance for only one position "You need both distances to compare. Calculate P₂ before you move to Step 4." Point to the compare checkbox at the end of Step 3.
Team skips Steps 4–6, jumps straight to Step 7 "Which start has lower wall risk? What did your coverage prediction show?" If they can't answer, send them back. Steps 4–6 are what make the decision defensible.
Robot behaves completely differently from Step 2 prediction "What did you expect? What actually happened? Write it in Step 10 and adjust." This is a good outcome — the gap between prediction and observation is the learning.
Team tries to start at (4, 2) or (8, 2) "Neither of those is open field. Pick a position your robot can actually occupy." No drama — redirect once and move on.
Team asks which starting position is best "That's the problem you're solving. Commit to two options and compare them."
Hard Stop Every team must have Steps 1–10 complete and a locked starting position with written justification before dismissal. Steps 14–16 can carry into Open Lab.
