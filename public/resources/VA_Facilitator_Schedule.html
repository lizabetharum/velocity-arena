Challenge Block 10:40–11:30 50 min
🛜 Digital
Endurance — Day 2 Endurance Allocation Stat Card
The formula goes on the board: P(t) = 100 - (20 - Endurance) x 1.5 x t.
Step 1 (5 min, full example): The facilitator works one complete example on the board. Endurance = 6. Decay rate = (20 - 6) x 1.5 x 1 = 21% per minute.
P(1) = 100 - 21 = 79%.
P(2) = 100 - 42 = 58%.
P(3) = 100 - 63 = 37%.
P(4) = 100 - 84 = 16%.
P(5) = 100 - 105 = -5% (capped at 0%).
This is the full worked example: every number shown, no steps skipped.
Step 2 (10 min, scaffolded): Each team calculates for Endurance = 4 using a partially completed table where the formula structure is filled in and only the arithmetic is blank.
Step 3 (10 min, independent): Each team calculates for Endurance = 2 with no scaffolding.
Step 4 (10 min): Teams plot all five t-values for their own Endurance allocation on paper. With Endurance = 4, the decay rate is 24% per minute. Power reaches 76% at t=1, 52% at t=2, 28% at t=3, 4% at t=4, and 0% at t=4 min 10 sec (t = 100/24 ≈ 4.167 min). What shape do you get? Compare your graph with the team next to you.
Step 5 (5 min): Two teams with different Endurance allocations read their t=3 values aloud. Different allocations produce different power levels at the same match minute. That comparison is where the formula becomes strategic, not just computational.

Facilitator Script
Challenge Block 10:30–11:20 50 min
🛜 Digital
Power Stat Challenge: Ratio and Motor Speed
The facilitator gives teams one rule: charge motor speed = Power points squared (written as Power² ) and follow through = POWER × 200 Teams calculate charge speed for Power = 1, 2, 4, and 8. Then: what is the ratio between Power 2 and Power 4? Between Power 4 and Power 8? SPEED is proportional (double the stat, double the output). The Power formula is not. Double the stat, quadruple the output divided by 2. That distinction is the mathematical core of Day 4. You test it physically: program Power 4 (16% speed) into MakeCode, place your bot exactly 40 cm from the ball on the start line, drive, and measure how far the ball travels. Then program Power 8 (64% speed) and repeat from the same start line.

Two-Formula Warm-Up

Formula Relay: All Five Types in One Race
Twenty problem cards, face down in a stack at the center of the table. The stack is shuffled so six formula types appear in random order: Speed proportionality (motor power = Speed / 20 x 100), Endurance decay (P(t) = 100 - (20 - E) x 1.5 x t), Turn ratio 0.2 + (TURNING x 0.02) Power squared, Follow through = POWER × 200, budget constraint (three stats given, find the fourth, confirm sum is 20), Proportional Scaling (2 scoop-scaling cards — students determine whether scaling one or both scoop dimensions is proportional). One person on the team flips the top card, the team solves it together, and brings the card and written work to the facilitator. Correct work gets a stamp and the next card is flipped. Incorrect work goes back to the table for one more attempt before moving on.First team to complete all 20 cards with fewer than 3 errors wins the round. Time is public and posted on the board. After the race, each team sorts their completed cards into six formula-type piles and counts errors per type. The formula type with the most errors is today's target: The team writes today's pre-match prediction using a calculation from their weakest formula type, not their strongest. The interleaving is what makes the self-diagnosis valid, and the race is what makes 20 problems feel like 5 minutes rather than 50. Prediction logged before the match. No exceptions. If they get all correct:
Option 1 — Pick the formula they're least confident about. No errors doesn't mean equal confidence. Ask them: "Which formula type do you understand the least, even if you got it right?" That's the spirit of LO 7.2 — self-diagnosis, not just error count.
Option 2 — Assign the hardest formula type. If a team had zero errors, the facilitator picks their formula for them — typically the distance formula or percent change, whichever is most complex. This keeps the prediction meaningful rather than letting them pick the easiest one. .

Teams apply the distance formula to their Season 1 match using the 8×4 field as a coordinate plane. Hook 10:05–10:35 30 min
🙌 Hands-On🛜 Digital
Stat Auction + Prediction Check
Twenty-five minutes of competitive stat bidding, then a 5-minute prediction check. This is the longest Hook in the program and it earns the time. Each team starts with 3 math tokens. Earn additional tokens by answering formula questions on the whiteboard: the facilitator posts one question per team in rotation, one token per correct answer, covering all four formula types. After two rounds of earning (roughly 8 minutes), the auction opens. Stat improvements are available in one-point increments. To bid, a team states the number of tokens they are offering and must immediately calculate the formula output for the new stat value out loud. "We bid 2 tokens for Speed plus 1. New motor power = 8 X 100 /20 = 40%." Incorrect formula output means the tokens are forfeited and the point goes to the next bidder. Valid bids win the stat point permanently for Season 2. The 25-minute window matters: short auctions produce one or two transactions and feel perfunctory. A full 25-minute window produces at least four to six bidding rounds per team. Real-time formula calculation under social pressure, with permanent stat consequences at stake, is the most authentically mathematical pressure in the program. Rushing it to 15 minutes cuts the moment before it lands. After the auction closes, teams look at their Season 2 prediction posted on the wall. Any team adjusting their win total must cite one data point from the dashboard. Write the justification on the card. This stays public through Friday.
